Base Scenario

The following examples use this scenario:

  • A completes work in 12 days.
  • B completes work in 16 days.

Initial Calculation:

  1. Total Work: LCM(12, 16) = 48 units
  2. A's Efficiency: units/day
  3. B's Efficiency: units/day

Solved Examples

1. Working Together from the Start

Question: If A and B work together, how many days will it take?

Solution:

  • Combined Efficiency (A + B) = 4 + 3 = 7 units/day
  • Time Taken = days = days

2. One Person Joins After a Delay

Question: A works for 4 days, then B joins. How long until completion?

Solution:

  • Work done by A in 4 days = 4 × 4 = 16 units
  • Remaining Work = 48 - 16 = 32 units
  • Time for remaining work = days
  • Total Time = 4 + days = days

3. One Person Leaves After Some Time

Question: A and B work together for 5 days, then A leaves. How long until completion?

Solution:

  • Work done in 5 days = 7 × 5 = 35 units
  • Remaining Work = 48 - 35 = 13 units
  • Time for B to finish = days = days
  • Total Time = 5 + days

4. Calculating Time for Remaining Work

Question: In the previous scenario, how many days will B take to complete the remaining work?

Solution:

  • Remaining Work = 13 units
  • Time for B = days = days

Advanced Scenarios & Shortcut Tricks

5. Leaving a Few Days Before Completion

Question: A and B work together, but A leaves 5 days before completion. How long does it take?

Method 1: Logical Deduction

  • B works alone for the last 5 days (15 units)
  • Work done together = 48 - 15 = 33 units
  • Time for initial work = days
  • Total time = days

Method 2: Shortcut Trick

  • A's missed work = 4 × 5 = 20 units
  • New Total Work = 48 + 20 = 68 units
  • Time Taken = days

6. Multiple People Leaving Before Completion

Question: A, B, and C start together. A leaves 5 days before, B leaves 4 days before completion. How long does it take?

Solution:

  • Total Work = LCM(12, 16, 24) = 48 units
  • Efficiencies: A = 4, B = 3, C = 2 units/day. Combined = 9 units/day
  • A's missed work = 5 × 4 = 20 units
  • B's missed work = 4 × 3 = 12 units
  • New Total Work = 48 + 20 + 12 = 80 units
  • Total Time = days

7. Alternate Day Working

Question: A (12 days) and B (16 days) work on alternate days, A starting first. How long will it take?

Solution:

  • Day 1 (A): 4 units
  • Day 2 (B): 3 units
  • Work Cycle (2 days): 7 units
  • 6 full cycles: 42 units, 12 days
  • Remaining Work: 6 units
  • Day 13 (A): 4 units done, 2 remaining
  • Day 14 (B): day
  • Total Time = 12 + 1 + days

8. Negative Work

Question: A builds in 12 days, B destroys in 16 days. How long until completion?

Solution:

  • A's Efficiency = +4 units/day
  • B's Efficiency = -3 units/day
  • Net Efficiency = +1 unit/day
  • Time Taken = days

The LCM Method - Step by Step

Step 1: Find LCM (Total Work Units)

For taking LCM: Find the smallest number that will be fully divided by all given individual completion times.

Step 2: Calculate Individual Efficiency

See what should be multiplied with each person's time to get the LCM - that number will be the efficiency of that person.

Formula: Efficiency = $LCM \div Individual Time$

Step 3: Apply Based on Scenario

  • Working together: Add all efficiencies
  • Working separately: Use individual efficiency
  • Alternating work: Calculate cycle-wise

Unit Digit Concept

The unit digit is the right-most digit of a number. When calculating the unit digit of a product or a number raised to a power, you often only need to consider the unit digits of the numbers involved.

Product Type Questions

To find the unit digit of a product of several numbers, multiply only their unit digits.

  • Example: For 278 * 623, multiply the unit digits 8 * 3 = 24. The resulting unit digit is 4.

Power Type Questions

To find the unit digit of a number in the form numberpower, the method depends on the unit digit of the base number.

Numbers ending in 0, 1, 5, or 6

For numbers with a unit digit of 0, 1, 5, or 6, the unit digit of the result will always be the same, regardless of the power.

  • Example: The unit digit of 4552145521 is 5.

Numbers ending in 4 or 9

These numbers have a cyclicity of two, meaning their unit digit pattern repeats every two powers. The unit digit depends on whether the power is odd or even.

Base Unit Digit Power Resulting Unit Digit
4 Odd 4
4 Even 6
9 Odd 9
9 Even 1
  • Example: For 24243, the base unit digit is 4 and the power is odd, so the resulting unit digit is 4.
  • Example: For 9998, the base unit digit is 9 and the power is even, so the resulting unit digit is 1.

Numbers ending in 2, 3, 7, or 8

These numbers have a cyclicity of four, meaning the unit digit pattern repeats every four powers.

To find the unit digit:

  1. Divide the power by 4 and find the remainder. For large powers, you only need to divide the last two digits of the power by 4 to find the remainder.
  2. Raise the base's unit digit to the power of the remainder. This will be the new unit digit.
  3. If the remainder is 0, use 4 as the power instead of 0.

  • Example: To find the unit digit of 9291:

    • Divide the power 91 by 4. The remainder is 3.
    • Calculate 23 = 8. The unit digit is 8.

  • Example: To find the unit digit of 127144:

    • Divide the last two digits of the power (44) by 4. The remainder is 0.
    • Since the remainder is 0, use the power 4. Calculate 74.
    • 74 = 2401. The unit digit is 1.

Divisibility Rules

Divisibility rules are shortcuts to determine if a number can be evenly divided by another, and they can also help in finding remainders.

Rules for Powers of 2 (2, 4, 8, 16, etc.)

The rule is based on the number of last digits.

  • Divisibility by 2: The last digit must be divisible by 2.
  • Divisibility by 4: The last two digits must be divisible by 4.
  • Divisibility by 8: The last three digits must be divisible by 8.

Rules for Powers of 5 (5, 25, 125, etc.)

This follows a similar pattern to the powers of 2.

  • Divisibility by 5: The last digit must be 0 or 5.
  • Divisibility by 25: The last two digits must be divisible by 25 (e.g., 00, 25, 50, 75).
  • Divisibility by 125: The last three digits must be divisible by 125.

Rules for 3 and 9

These rules are based on the sum of the digits.

  • Divisibility by 3: The sum of all digits must be divisible by 3.

  • Divisibility by 9: The sum of all digits must be divisible by 9.

    • Shortcut for 9: Pair up digits that sum to 9 and cancel them out. If all digits are cancelled, the number is divisible by 9.

Rule for 6

A number is divisible by 6 if it is divisible by both 2 and 3.

Rule for 11

This rule uses the alternating sum of digits.

  1. Sum the digits in the odd positions (1st, 3rd, 5th, etc.).
  2. Sum the digits in the even positions (2nd, 4th, 6th, etc.).
  3. Find the difference between the two sums.
  4. If the difference is 0 or a multiple of 11, the number is divisible by 11.

  • Example: For the number 1331:

    • Odd positions sum: 1 + 3 = 4.
    • Even positions sum: 3 + 1 = 4.
    • Difference: 4 - 4 = 0. The number is divisible by 11.
